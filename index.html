Venezuela Shortage Crisis

How did a country with one of the largest reserves of oil in the world turn into a starving nation?

Around the world, the country of Venezuela was famous for its large oil reserves, beautiful women, stunning landscapes, agriculture, and distinct natural regions. Today, Venezuela is known for its shortage of basic goods such as food, medicine, water, and electricity. It is also notorious for its hyperinflation, corrupt government, and one of the highest crime rates in the world. According to the Freedom House, an organization that reports on levels of democracy around the world, Venezuela has been rated as a not free country with one of the worst rates for freedom, political rights, and civil liberties.

Venezuela’s crisis started when the global prices for oil fell from $111 per barrel in 2014 to $27 per barrel in 2016. The fall of the oil cause great economic chaos in Venezuela because 95 % of the exports are oil. This led to a critical economic and political crisis that transformed into extreme food and medicine shortages, rising crimes rate, unemployment, massive protests, and hyperinflation rates in the economy. Venezuela’s economic disaster is the compilation of years of economic mismanagement and the current fall of the barrel of oil in the market. The International Energy Statistics has measure the decrease of petroleum production in Venezuela.

For those who decide to stay, they have to deal with the economic crisis that is drowning Venezuelan. The company Statista shows projections of Venezuelan inflation rates of 3,474.73% for 2019. The crisis has destroyed the currency of its country, making it almost nonexistent. Many of its citizens have started using the US dollars in order to have any purchasing power. In January 2018, the monthly income in Venezuela was 1,307,646 bolivars per month, which is the equivalent of $6.13, according CNNMoney. On January 25, 2018, the basic family basket in Venezuela had surpassed 16-million bolivars. To cover the cost of the basic, family basket a person would need 93 times the monthly income, according to El Nacional.

Venezuela’s economic crisis has led to a sky-high inflation and deficiencies of food, medicines, and basic necessities like toilet paper, soap, toothpaste, and shampoo. Professionals’ opinions state that the government’s strict price controls, which were meant to keep basic goods affordable, are slightly to blame. In the country, several manufactures have decided to reduce production because of the limitations there are to charge for their products. Imports fell, making foreign-made goods too expensive to purchase. Various buyers were faced with the choice of waiting in line for basic goods or paying for overpriced goods that were being re-sold by bachaqueros or black-market traffickers.

Another economic issue is the currency control. It was first introduced by Chavez in 2003 to control US dollars in Venezuela. The government implemented the currency control by selling U.S dollars at different rates. They created a black market and enhanced the opportunities for corruption. For instance, a business that was authorized to buy dollars at preferential rates to purchase basic goods like food and medicine could instead sell those dollars for a higher rate to a third party. In December 2017, the official exchange rate was 10 bolivars to the dollar, while the black-market rate was more than 9000 bolivars to the dollar.

The majority of the population is going hungry due to the insufficiency of food and hyper-inflated prices. Around 64.3% of the people surveyed said that they lost 11 kilograms in 2017. The lack of food products has had a dramatic impact on Venezuelans’ weights and on what they eat, according to the survey life conditions, conducted by Simón Bolivar University, Andrés Bello Catholic University, and the Central University of Venezuela.

The government of Venezuela executed the law of Fair Prices to keep the cost of products inexpensive. This law caused that many companies to lose their revenues, which also caused them to stop manufacturing their products, which made the shortage worse. As a result, Venezuelans have turned to expensive imports or buying on the black market. For many citizens, basic items like eggs and rice have become overpriced, and they cannot afford it. Venezuela’s monthly minimum wage in US dollars is equivalent to $ 8. Venezuelans making the monthly minimum wage can only afford 6% of the basic basket of goods. In 2016, the Venezuelan Pharmaceutical Federation estimated that 85 % of basic medicines were unavailable or hard to obtain. Hospitals have a lack of supplies such as antibiotics, morphine, pain killers, gauze, and soap, among others. Diseases previously eliminated from the country have reemerged such as, cholera, diphtheria, malaria, hepatitis A, tuberculosis, yellow fever, and typhoid, among others. In 2016, infant mortality increased by 30 % and maternal mortality by 65 % over two years prior, according to governmental figures. Patients are accountable for any material, required to treat their illnesses. When patients manage to get the money together to purchase the medical items, they become objects of thieves looking to steal and resell medication on the black market.

Today, 87 % of Venezuelans are living in poverty, according to the universities studies conducted by Simón Bolivar University, Andrés Bello Catholic University, and the Central University of Venezuela. This is due to the fact that companies cannot compete with the regulated prices that the government offers. So, they either decide to close or work with as little personnel as possible, causing the levels of unemployment and levels of crime to rise.

The absence of opportunities and the rise of worsening poverty have aggravated Venezuela’s high rates of violence. In 2016, the city of Caracas, Venezuela saw its highest-ever homicide rate, at 130.35 % homicides per 100,000 residents, according to Statista.
